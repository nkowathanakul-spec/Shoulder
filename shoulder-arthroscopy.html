01

Anatomy & Portals

Fewer landmarks and more muscle than the knee. Mark landmarks before distending the joint.

  • Posterior — primary, made first. 1.5–2 cm inferior + 1–2 cm medial to posterolateral acromion, aim at coracoid. 1 cm medial → parallel to glenoid.
  • Anterior — made second, under direct vision. Through soft-spot triangle (biceps / subscapularis / glenoid). Stay lateral to coracoid, superior to subscapularis.
  • Low anterior — passes ~1 cm from cephalic vein = closest portal to any neurovascular structure.
  • Lateral — subacromial workhorse. ~3 cm lateral to acromion; axillary n. ~5 cm distal.
  • Neviaser (superior) — 1 cm medial to medial acromion, 30–45°. Suture retrieval; SSN ~3 cm medial.
Structures at risk
  • Suprascapular n. — <2 cm medial to glenoid
  • Axillary n. — ≥3 cm from most portals
  • Subclavian vein — <1 cm from 5-o'clock portal (percutaneous only)

Full detail — establishing portals

Posterior first. Enter the soft spot with an 18-gauge spinal needle, a finger on the coracoid to aim anteromedially, and preinsufflate the joint with 30–40 mL of saline — free flow and free backflow confirm you are intracapsular. Distention lifts the humeral head off the glenoid and eases cannula entry.

Anterior second. Made outside-in (spinal-needle localization) or inside-out (Wissinger rod), through the soft-spot triangle. The musculocutaneous nerve usually runs 3–5 cm below the coracoid tip — staying lateral to the coracoid and above the subscapularis keeps the brachial plexus and axillary vessels out of harm's way.

Accessory. The 7-o'clock portal (Davidson & Rivenburgh) reaches the inferior capsule; the lateral portal is angled toward the tuberosity to open the bursa; the Neviaser portal is bounded by clavicle, acromion, scapular spine and the posterosuperior glenoid rim.

02

Diagnostic Arthroscopy

15-point circuit · lateral decubitus · glenoid horizontal.

Laxity signs

  • Peel-back — biceps out of traction
  • Drive-through sign
  • Rotator interval >1.5 cm

Don't over-repair variants

  • Sublabral hole & Buford complex ≠ Bankart
  • True Bankart extends below the equator
  • Above 3–9 → cuff / biceps · Below → instability

Humeral side

  • Hill-Sachs interval → on-track vs off-track
  • Finish in subacromial bursa (view M)
Vision
  • SBP ≤30 mmHg above pump + epinephrine in bag
Full detail — the systematic circuit

Run the same circuit every time: start at the anterior soft spot and follow the labrum from the biceps anchor around to the humeral capsular insertion and back. The labrum is divided into six zones — lesions above the 3-to-9 equator implicate cuff or biceps, those below it implicate instability.

The Buford complex is a cord-like middle glenohumeral ligament inserting directly into the biceps anchor with an absent anterosuperior labrum — a normal variant that must not be sutured. On-track vs off-track is judged by measuring the Hill-Sachs interval against the glenoid track using the bare-area method.

Finish outside the joint: remove all glenohumeral distention, redirect the posterior cannula under the acromion, and bursectomize to expose the cuff insertion at the tuberosity.

03

Subacromial Impingement

Anterolateral pain, worse overhead. Extrinsic (acromion / CA lig / AC joint) vs intrinsic (age, vascularity).

Imaging

  • Grashey AP → critical shoulder angle, AC osteophytes
  • Axillary lateral → os acromiale
  • Outlet view → acromial morphology

Acromion — Bigliani / Morrison

  • Type I flat · II curved · III hooked
  • Type III → highest cuff pathology, least outlet
  • Acquired CA spurs ≠ native shape (spurs ↑ with age)
Critical shoulder angle
  • >35° → ↑ shear, cuff strain, degenerative tears

Surgery (after failed conservative Rx)

  • Bursectomy · CA-ligament release · acromioplasty ± distal clavicle
  • Acromioplasty efficacy debated
Full detail — impingement & the critical shoulder angle

Whether extrinsic compression or intrinsic degeneration drives cuff disease is unsettled — both contribute. The critical shoulder angle captures two independent drivers in one measurement: glenoid inclination and lateral acromial extension, taken on a true AP between the glenoid margins and the most inferolateral acromion.

Nicholson showed acromial bone spurs accumulate with age while native morphology stays fixed — so a "hook" may be an acquired CA-ligament spur rather than a true type III. Gerber's lateral acromioplasty aims to lower a high CSA to protect a cuff repair, but the evidence is early, and the value of acromioplasty itself remains debated and decided case-by-case on imaging.

Full detail — fixation, healing & irreparable tears

Footprint is the crux. Single-row restores roughly half; double-row and transosseous-equivalent restore nearly all, and resist gapping best along the anterior edge where the rotator cable inserts. Small-to-medium tears heal comparably with either; large-to-massive tears favour DR/TE with higher MRI healing.

Cost & selection. Single-row is cheapest per case, but double-row is increasingly more cost-effective through fewer retears. Best healing: under 70, acute tears under 3 cm, a healthy interface, and compliance with 6–9 months of rehab. Failure loads onto larger and retracted tears, advanced Goutallier grade, smoking, osteoporosis, diabetes, and overly aggressive rehab.

Retracted tears. U- and L-shaped tears (Lo-Burkhart) are shrunk with margin convergence before fixing to bone; Goutallier III–IV with a stump under 15 mm and a positive tangent sign is effectively irreparable.

Instability — Bankart & SLAP

Soft-tissue repair works when bone loss is modest.

Anterior — Bankart

  • Anteroinferior labrum, below equator
  • Variants: bony · ALPSA · GLAD · HAGL

Full detail — repair technique & the bone threshold

Anchor technique. Seat at least three anchors 1–2 mm onto the articular surface, restore inferior glenohumeral ligament tension, and eliminate the drive-through sign; knotless constructs avoid knot impingement on the cuff or head. Add a rotator-interval closure in the hyperlax or contact athlete.

Bone is the ceiling. Glenoid loss beyond 20–25% or an off-track Hill-Sachs points to Latarjet or bony augmentation, with remplissage for an engaging Hill-Sachs.

SLAP mechanism. Lesions arise from traction and, in throwers, torsional peel-back of the posterosuperior labrum during late cocking. Because results are poor in older patients, repair is reserved for symptomatic younger athletes; tenodesis or tenotomy is favoured with age.

06

AC Joint & Distal Clavicle

Resect only the symptomatic joint — and only just enough.

Indication

  • Cross-body adduction pain + tenderness
  • Lidocaine block 80–100% relief predicts success
  • Asymptomatic (even if arthritic on X-ray) → don't resect

Technique — Mumford

  • Direct vs indirect → Campbell prefers indirect
  • Direct may injure superior capsule → instability
How much
  • Resect 5–8 mm (5 mm corrects abutment)
  • Over-resection → horizontal instability
  • Preserve posterior + superior capsule; two-pin gauge
Full detail — confirming the pain generator & resection

Confirm before resecting. Point tenderness, pain on cross-body adduction, and 80–100% relief from an intra-articular lidocaine block. Adding distal clavicle resection to a cuff repair gives no benefit when the AC joint is asymptomatic — even if it looks arthritic on film.

Indirect Mumford. Levine's long-term series and cadaver work found no added benefit to the direct approach, which risks the superior capsular ligaments and distal-clavicle instability. Aim for 5–8 mm — 5 mm alone corrects abutment — protect the posterior and superior capsule, and gauge width with the two-pin technique.

Neurovascular Safety

Most complications are transient — from traction, position, and fluid.

Quadrilateral space

  • Borders: teres minor / teres major / triceps long head / humeral neck
  • Contents: axillary n. + posterior circumflex humeral a.

Nerve injury

  • Usually transient traction neurapraxia
  • Paresthesia ~30% (Paulos) · ~10% (Klein)
  • Best view (30° FF / 70° abd) = most plexus strain
Fluid extravasation
  • > knee · avg gain ~8.7 lb
  • Tight portals · don't breach deltoid fascia · minimal pump pressure

Full detail — traction, position & fluid

Nerve injury. Usually a transient neurapraxia from traction or malpositioning rather than the blade. Paulos and Franklin reported ~30% transient paresthesia with traction, Klein ~10%; the position that gives the best view (about 30° forward flexion, 70° abduction) also loads the brachial plexus most — balance exposure against strain and keep landmarks marked.

Fluid extravasation. Exceeds the knee because more tissue is traversed — Lo and Burkhart measured ~8.7 lb average weight gain — and is worst during extraarticular work like subacromial decompression. Keep portals tight, avoid violating the deltoid fascia, and raise pump pressure only when necessary.
